Stone houses for the gods, wooden houses for mortals

 

The people of Angkor built thatch houses on stilts, just as many houses in the Cambodian countryside are today. There are a lot of good reasons to do this in a humid and rainy country. If you are living near a river, you are safe above the floodwaters in the rainy season, and in the dry season, cool air can circulate under your floor. Most of all, you can sleep easier knowing that scorpions, snakes and prowling tigers can't wander straight into your bedroom!

Their houses were very light and built without nails, so most Cambodians could quickly pack up their home and move it down the road if they needed to! Stones and bricks were kept for the temples of the gods. Even the king lived in a palace made of wood, but it was much more permanent than the houses of ordinary Khmer people. Zhou Daguan writes that the royal buildings were big and richly ornamented. They had lead-tiled roofs, soaring beams and pillars.

What is an orbit?

Does gravity always pull objects together? It does, but this doesn't always result in objects colliding with each other. If objects are set in motion, then they can orbit one another. That means they circle around each other.

A satellite travels so fast horizontally that instead of crashing to Earth, the satellite ends up circling the Earth instead. The Planets in our Solar System have orbited the Sun for billions of years!
