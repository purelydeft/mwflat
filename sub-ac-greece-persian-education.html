Xenophon, a young Athenian who lived at the end of the 5th century BCE, was a man of many talents. As an army general, he led 10,000 Greek soldiers from Persia, where they had been left stranded, all the way back to Greece. Xenophon was a controversial figure. He disliked democracy and admired the Persians who lived under the absolute rule of their Great King.

Xenophon spent time in the service of the Persian king. He wrote a work called Cyropaedia about the education of Cyrus, a prominent Persian king. According to Xenophon, Persian noblemen like Cyrus learned only three things throughout their lives: to ride a horse, to use a bow and arrow, and to say the truth.
