Markets and Trade ... but no Money

 

Far from the sea and the Mekong river, Angkor was an inland empire that built its wealth through rich rice harvests. It wasn't a port city where riches were made through trade. The capital was hard to get to whichever way you went - overland or upstream - but Zhou Daguan said there were Chinese traders in the markets at Angkor. There were many Cambodian goods the Chinese wanted and they exchanged them for items that the Angkorians desired from China.

Zhou was interested in the quality of Cambodian products such as ivory, rhinoceros horns, kingfisher feathers and beeswax. He saw that the Cambodians valued Chinese goods such as silks, ceramics, fine-toothed combs, writing paper and copper dishes. But at Angkor they didn't use money, so how would you buy these things? Well, you would barter (or swap)! Angkorian's bartered with rice, cloth or gold and silver.

What is an orbit?

Does gravity always pull objects together? It does, but this doesn't always result in objects colliding with each other. If objects are set in motion, then they can orbit one another. That means they circle around each other.

A satellite travels so fast horizontally that instead of crashing to Earth, the satellite ends up circling the Earth instead. The Planets in our Solar System have orbited the Sun for billions of years!
