Hiding in mountains

 

What do Mt Fuji in Japan, Popocatépetl in Mexico and Mt St Helens in the United States of America all have in common? They are active volcanoes on dry land! They are also all stratovolcanoes, which are steep-sided volcanoes.

It's had three major eruptions. The last one was about 640,000 years ago. So you might think it must be dead by now. Wrong. It might be getting ready to erupt again.

Volcanoes on dry land usually form because they're on plates that are being pushed together, with one sliding below the other, known as subduction. When a plate sinks down, bits come off it, like water. The water can cause part of the overlying mantle to melt. The magma then rises and erupts at the surface, forming a volcano! These volcanoes often hide in the middle of a mountain range, like Mt St Helens volcano does within the Cascade Mountains in north-west USA.

We are all made of atoms, and the different kinds of atoms are called elements. Oxygen, hydrogen, carbon, iron and gold are all elements.

In our daily lives, elements remain unchanged. You cannot easily turn hydrogen into helium or carbon into oxygen.

But in the super hot and super dense core of a star, hydrogen atoms are fused together to become helium atoms. This is nuclear fusion, and this process powers all stars including our Sun.
